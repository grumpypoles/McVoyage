Day 01

Dublin to Portrush

12-Jul-2023

Belfast - Embarking on our much-anticipated 11-day bus tour, we eagerly met our fellow travelers and our knowledgeable driver/tour guide.
As we journeyed towards Belfast, our guide provided insights into the significance of July 12th in Northern Ireland. This date marks the anniversary of the Battle of the Boyne in 1690, a pivotal event in the Williamite War in Ireland. The association with the Protestant Orange Order adds layers of historical and cultural complexity to this date. Interestingly, our guide shared a sense of unease about driving a vehicle with UE number plates in Northern Ireland, highlighting the historical and political sensitivities of the region.
Our first stop brought us to the Solidarity Wall, a powerful symbol of the Troubles - the ethno-nationalist conflict that gripped Northern Ireland from the late 1960s until the Good Friday Agreement in 1998. The wall, adorned with murals reflecting the troubled history of the region, served as a poignant reminder of the challenges faced by the communities during that tumultuous period.
Continuing our exploration, we were captivated by the impressive Titanic Museum in Belfast. Nestled in the city that birthed the ill-fated ship, the museum showcased a treasure trove of interactive exhibits, artifacts, and reconstructions. From the construction of the Titanic to its tragic maiden voyage in 1912, the museum offered a comprehensive narrative. We delved into the world of shipbuilding in Belfast, learned about the individuals who played a role in the Titanic's creation, and gained insights into the lives of the passengers. The exhibits painted a vivid picture of the sinking and the aftermath, creating a deep connection with the historical tragedy.

Continuing our journey towards Portrush, our exploration unfolded with a breathtaking stop at the Portaneevy Viewing Point. From this elevated vantage, we marveled at the expansive views of Rathlin, Carrick-a-rede, and Sheep Island. The landscape, adorned with natural beauty, set the stage for the wonders that awaited us on this leg of the tour.
In the distance, the iconic Carrick-a-Rede Rope Bridge came into view. This renowned tourist attraction suspended itself boldly across a 20-meter-wide chasm, connecting the mainland to the tiny Carrick Island near the village of Ballintoy. The nerve-wracking 30-meter height above the rocks and sea below added an exhilarating touch to the picturesque surroundings.
Our next stop brought us to the awe-inspiring Giant's Causeway, a natural marvel on the northeast coast of Northern Ireland. This geological wonder comprised around 40,000 interlocking basalt columns, predominantly hexagonal in shape. Formed some 50 to 60 million years ago through volcanic activity, these columns extended seamlessly from the cliffs into the sea, creating a captivating sight that spoke of the Earth's ancient processes.
Further enriching our journey, we explored the historic Dunluce Castle. Built in the 16th century by the MacDonnell clan, a prominent Gaelic Irish family, the castle stood as a testament to the region's rich history. Serving as the seat of the Clan McDonnell of Antrim, the castle's ruins resonated with tales of the past, transporting us to a bygone era.
As dusk settled, we found respite at the Mandalay B&B in Portrush. The hospitality was matched only by the excellence of the breakfast served the next morning, featuring a delightful touch of local flavor with porridge accompanied by Drambuie.

Day 02

Portrush to Donegal

13-Jul-2023

Our expedition from Portrush to Donegal unfolded as an enthralling odyssey through Northern Ireland's tapestry of history and awe-inspiring landscapes. Commencing our adventure in the venerable city of Derry, we embarked on an enchanting promenade along the impeccably preserved city walls. These bastions, among the most intact in Europe, afforded us a portal into the annals of time, bestowing a distinctive insight into the city's storied past. Derry's four original gates — Bishop's Gate, Shipquay Gate, Ferryquay Gate, and Butcher Gate—stood as taciturn sentinels, each concealing a compelling narrative of the city's evolution. As we meandered through these gates, the narratives of the Troubles, a tumultuous era etched into Northern Ireland's history, captivated our senses. The dichotomy in the nomenclature, with nationalists favoring Derry and unionists adhering to Londonderry, underscored the profound identity and political nuances characterizing the region. Our sojourn in Derry progressed to the Derry Guildhall, an opulent neo-Gothic creation envisioned by architect Albert Ernest Richardson. Completed in 1890, the Guildhall's towering clock was a testament to the architectural brilliance of its era. Stepping into its interior, we found ourselves immersed in a tapestry of grandeur, a harmonious fusion of historical resonance and artistic finesse.
Transitioning from urban allure to the embrace of nature, our next destination beckoned: Glenveagh Castle, ensconced within the sprawling expanse of Glenveagh National Park. Encompassing 40,000 acres of mountains, lakes, and forests, this park, the second-largest in Ireland, unfolded a mesmerizing panorama. Glenveagh Castle, a testament to 19th-century architectural trends with its Scottish Baronial style, stood as a picturesque marvel amidst the park's natural splendor.

Later that day, we found ourselves enchanted by the pristine beauty of Narin, an Irish gem nestled along the Atlantic coast. Aptly named An Fhearthainn in Irish, Narin boasts an expansive beach adorned with golden-white sands stretching over a mile. The beach is embraced by gentle low dunes, creating a picturesque backdrop that invites leisurely strolls and moments of tranquil contemplation.
As we ventured further along the Atlantic coast, the majestic Sliabh Liag, or Slieve League, beckoned us with its awe-inspiring presence. This towering mountain is renowned for hosting some of the highest sea cliffs in Europe, providing unparalleled views of the vast Atlantic Ocean. Rising up to an astonishing 601 meters, the cliffs at Sliabh Liag are nearly three times the height of the more famous Cliffs of Moher. Our hearts raced as we stood at the Bunglass viewing point, absorbing the breathtaking scenery that unfolded before us. The rugged cliffs, the roaring ocean below, and the distant horizon created a visual symphony that left an indelible mark on our journey.
Donegal, a county located in the northwest of Ireland, became a captivating tapestry of landscapes and cultural richness. It proudly stands as the northernmost county in the Republic of Ireland, sharing a border with Northern Ireland. Donegal, the county town, welcomed us with its historical charm and vibrant atmosphere, serving as a gateway to the wonders that unfolded in this northern corner of the Emerald Isle. With each step, Donegal revealed its unique character, inviting us to explore its natural wonders, cultural heritage, and the warmth of its people.

Day 03

Donegal to Westport

14-Jul-2023

Our journey from Donegal to Westport was a captivating exploration of history and natural beauty, unveiling the rich tapestry of Ireland's past. Our first stop, Classiebawn Castle, echoed with tales of both grandeur and tragedy. Built in the 19th century, the original castle met a fiery demise in 1929, only to be reborn in the 1960s as a neo-Gothic masterpiece by Sir Basil Spence. Commissioned by the Mountbatten family, it served as their summer retreat until 1979 when the castle gained global attention due to the tragic assassination of Lord Louis Mountbatten and three others by the IRA.
Our journey continued to the enchanting Glencar Waterfall, a picturesque cascade near Manorhamilton. Nature unfolded its splendor as the waterfall descended 15 meters down a rocky outcrop, creating a mesmerizing spectacle. The serene surroundings and the rhythmic sound of falling water transported us to a tranquil oasis, emphasizing the raw beauty that Ireland effortlessly offers.
Carrowmore, our next destination, carried us back in time to the Neolithic period. This complex of prehistoric passage tombs, nestled in County Sligo, is one of Ireland's largest and oldest megalithic cemeteries. Dating back over 5,000 years, the tombs bear witness to the establishment of farming communities during a pivotal era in Ireland's history. The ancient stones and mysterious burial sites left us in awe, contemplating the lives and rituals of those who lived millennia ago.

Our next destination was the National Museum of Ireland - Country Life, situated amid the enchanting surroundings of Turlough Park House, an 18th-century mansion. Here, we delved into the diverse aspects of rural life, where farming, fishing, hunting, housing, and traditional crafts were intricately intertwined. The museum's noteworthy exhibits unveiled the nuances of traditional farming practices, emphasized the central role of homes in rural life, and explored the profound impact of the Irish Famine on rural communities. The museum's dedication to preserving and showcasing Ireland's countryside material culture was evident through its comprehensive displays.
Continuing our journey, we ventured to Achill Island, the largest island off Ireland's west coast. Despite encountering heavy rain that limited our time on the island, we were captivated by its breathtaking landscapes and rugged coastline. The Michael Davitt Bridge, linking Achill Island to the mainland, served as the entrance to this natural wonder, offering glimpses of Ireland's untamed beauty.
Our final destination was Westport, a town steeped in 18th-century history and masterfully designed by the renowned James Wyatt. The architectural elegance of Westport House, a publicly accessible stately home, provided insights into the town's rich past. A leisurely stroll along the Mall, a tree-lined area beside the Carrowbeg River, allowed us to absorb the historical ambiance of this captivating town.

Day 04

Around Galway County

15-Jul-2023

Our journey through Galway County was a tapestry woven with historical richness, natural beauty, and poignant tales of the past.
One of the first stops on our itinerary was the enchanting Doolough Valley, a place where the breathtaking landscapes hide a tragic tale from Ireland's history. The valley, adorned by the meandering River Dooagh, served witness to the heart-wrenching events of the Great Famine. On March 30, 1849, a group of destitute people undertook a grueling journey from Doolough Valley to the village of Louisburgh, miles away, to prove their need for government assistance. The arduous trek claimed several lives, exposing the callousness of some officials during that devastating period. The Doolough Tragedy remains etched in memory as a symbol of the suffering endured by the Irish population during one of their darkest hours.
As we continued our exploration, Aasleagh Falls emerged as a hidden gem nestled on the River Erriff, near the northern end of Killary Harbour – Ireland's sole fjord. Unlike a singular, thunderous cascade, Aasleagh Falls presented itself as a series of cascades and rapids, each frame capturing the raw beauty of nature's force. The falls, with the backdrop of Killary Harbour's majestic fjord, painted a picture of serenity and untamed wilderness.

Our next destination, Kylemore Abbey, transported us back to the Victorian era. The sprawling castle, constructed in the late 1800s as a private residence for the wealthy Dr. Mitchell Henry, stood proudly on the shores of Pollacapall Lough in Connemara. The architectural marvel, a blend of Gothic Revival elements, was surrounded by meticulously landscaped gardens, adding to the grandeur of the estate. The history of Kylemore Abbey took a turn in 1920 when it became a Benedictine monastery, a role it still fulfills today. The presence of the Benedictine nuns, amid the historical walls and picturesque surroundings, imparts a sense of continuity and spirituality to this remarkable place.
Our journey took us to Killary Fjord, a breathtaking inlet straddling the borders of Galway and Mayo. Renowned as Ireland's only true fjord, Killary Fjord stretched inland for 16 kilometers, reaching the charming village of Leenane. The surrounding landscape, dominated by towering mountains like Connacht's highest peak, Mweelrea, creates a dramatic and awe-inspiring backdrop.
Our journey through Galway County also led us to Derrigimlagh, a remote bog with a pivotal role in the history of wireless telegraphy. In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the Italian inventor Guglielmo Marconi chose this desolate landscape for his groundbreaking experiments. The flat terrain and isolation of Derrigimlagh facilitated the installation of large antenna systems. In 1907, Marconi established a wireless telegraphy station at Derrigimlagh, from where, in 1909, he achieved a historic milestone by successfully transmitting the first transatlantic wireless message to Glace Bay in Nova Scotia, Canada. The echoes of Marconi's groundbreaking work still linger in the boggy expanse of Derrigimlagh, a testament to the convergence of innovation and nature in this remote corner of County Galway.

Day 05

Westport to Galway City

16-Jul-2023

Our journey from Westport to Galway City was a memorable expedition, marked by the end of our adventure with a charismatic driver/guide whose background as a former radio DJ added a unique twist to our travel experience. His organizational skills and vast knowledge were evident as he seamlessly switched between captivating stories and a carefully curated playlist of music, creating an engaging atmosphere. However, there were moments when the constant transition between narratives and tunes became a bit overwhelming for some of us.
Our first stop on this eventful day was the historic Ballintubber Abbey, a religious marvel situated in County Mayo. The abbey, officially known as "St. Mary's Augustinian Abbey, Ballintubber," holds a significant place in Irish history, dating back to its foundation in 1216 by King Cathal Crobhdearg Ua Conchobair. What makes this abbey truly unique is that it is the only church in Ireland founded by an Irish king that remains in active use to this day.
Continuing our journey, we headed to Loch Na Fooey, a glacial lake in County Galway. It's about 2.5 miles long and 0.5 miles wide, surrounded by the Galway mountains to the south and Mayo's Partry Mountains to the north. On the lake's western end, there's a small sandy shore that adds to the scenic appeal. Loch Na Fooey is more than just a pretty sight; it's known for the ancient "Finny volcano," formed around 490 million years ago when the Iapetus Ocean closed, causing the merging of Ireland's two halves. Standing by the lake, you can appreciate the ancient geological processes that shaped the landscape and the enduring beauty of the area.

Galway City, nestled on Ireland's west coast, proved to be a captivating stopover on our journey. The Nox Hotel, our accommodation on the city's outskirts, set the stage for an exploration that seamlessly blended cultural immersion with leisurely discovery. As we disembarked from the bus, the prospect of stretching our legs and delving into Galway's charm beckoned. A brisk walk towards the city center became a welcome respite after hours on the road, and Galway unfolded as a tapestry of vibrant culture, rich history, and artistic expression. Known as the "Cultural Heart of Ireland," Galway exuded a warmth that belied its status as the fourth largest city in the country. The city's commitment to the arts was palpable, evident in events such as the Galway International Arts Festival, the Galway Film Fleadh, and the Galway Oyster Festival. Our fortunate timing aligned with the city's cultural fervor, allowing us to immerse ourselves in its lively atmosphere.
The Spanish Arch, an iconic landmark along the River Corrib, provided a glimpse into Galway's historical narrative. Originally an extension of the city walls, the arch stood as a testament to the city's resilience and pride in its past. Galway Bay, with its breathtaking landscapes, invited us to appreciate the beauty of the water, offering a serene backdrop for leisurely strolls and recreational activities.
The Latin Quarter, a historic area characterized by narrow medieval streets and colorful storefronts, added a charming layer to our experience. Wandering through its streets felt like a journey through time, with each corner whispering tales of a bygone era. The Quarter's array of shops, pubs, and restaurants contributed to the city's dynamic character.

Day 06

Galway City to Killarney

17-Jul-2023

Our journey from Galway City to Killarney commenced with anticipation as we were picked up from our hotel by a new driver/guide, ready to unveil the treasures that awaited us.
Our first delightful pause was at Hazel Mountain Chocolate, a hidden gem nestled in Oughtmama, at the foothills of the Burren Mountains along the Wild Atlantic Way. This bean-to-bar chocolate factory, one of the smallest and most remote in the world, charmed us with its rustic allure. Surrounded by the Burren hills, the factory crafted Irish chocolate directly from sustainable cacao beans. The aroma of cocoa filled the air as we indulged in the rich flavors, topping up our energy reserves for the impending visit to the iconic Cliffs of Moher.
Energized and eager, we ventured next to Corcomroe Abbey, also known as St. Mary's Abbey, a medieval Cistercian monastery founded in 1194 by Domnall Mór Ua Briain, King of Thomond. The abbey's name, "Corcomroe," resonated with the Irish "Cúl Trá Mhór," translating to "great stony place" - a fitting descriptor for its location in the rugged Burren region. Exploring the abbey's weathered stones and ancient arches, we sensed the weight of centuries gone by.
Continuing our journey through time, our next stop unveiled the Poulnabrone Dolmen, a renowned megalithic monument standing proudly in County Clare. This Neolithic structure, dating back over 5,000 years to around 4200 BCE, fascinated us with its simplicity and mystery. The dolmen, resembling a table made of large flat stones supported by smaller ones, stood as a testament to the ingenuity of our ancient ancestors.

Embarking on the next leg of our journey, we found ourselves standing in awe at the edge of Ireland's iconic Cliffs of Moher. Renowned for their breathtaking beauty and dramatic coastal scenery, these natural wonders stretch for approximately 14 kilometers along the western coast, reaching a staggering height of 214 meters above the tumultuous Atlantic Ocean. Composed of layers of shale and sandstone, these cliffs form a geological masterpiece dating back to the Namurian period, a staggering 325 million years ago. As luck would have it, the weather smiled upon us, allowing us to immerse ourselves in the splendor of the cliffs for an extended period. The generous sunshine illuminated the rugged terrain, offering panoramic views that prompted us to capture the magic through hundreds of photographs.
Our journey unfolded further as we navigated the waters of the Shannon Estuary, crossing from Killimer to Tarbert aboard the Killimer ferry. The gentle sway of the boat mirrored the anticipation building within us as we ventured toward our next destination. The Kingfisher Lodge, a welcoming haven nestled in the heart of southwestern Ireland, awaited our arrival, promising three nights of comfort as we explored the enchanting landscapes that lay ahead.
Upon reaching Killarney, a town steeped in charm and nestled near the serene Killarney lakes, we found ourselves approximately 70 kilometers north-northwest of Cork. Not only is Killarney a picturesque destination, but it also marks the beginning and end of the famed Ring of Kerry, a scenic drive that unfolds through some of the most captivating landscapes in Ireland. The town, surrounded by natural splendor, boasts attractions such as Ross Castle, Muckross House, and the expansive Killarney National Park.

Day 07

Around Dingle Peninsula

18-Jul-2023

Our day unfolded with the promise of exploration as we set out to traverse the captivating Dingle Peninsula, a gem nestled in County Kerry, Ireland. The plan was simple yet enticing - to drive around the peninsula, immersing ourselves in its breathtaking landscapes and enjoy sighteeing.
Our first stop, Inch Beach, unfolded as a panoramic canvas of natural beauty. Stretching approximately 3 miles, it claimed the title of Ireland's longest beach. The sandy expanse, framed by Dingle Bay and the Kerry mountain ranges, offered not only a visual feast but also a playground for water sports enthusiasts.
Continuing our journey, we found ourselves in the charming town of Dingle, perched on the peninsula's edge. The town bore witness to the close bond between its people and Fungie, the beloved Dingle Dolphin. The next destination on our Dingle Peninsula odyssey was Blaskets View, a viewing platform in Dún Chaoin. From this vantage point, we were treated to commanding views of the Blasket Islands, a cluster of isles off the west coast.
As the day wound down, we concluded our Dingle Peninsula escapade with a pleasant walk around Killarney National Park. The serene landscapes and tranquil ambiance provided a perfect counterpoint to the exhilarating experiences of the day.

Day 08

Around County Kerry

19-Jul-2023

The day began at the Aghadoe Overlook, a scenic viewpoint offering panoramic views of the surrounding countryside, the Lakes of Killarney, and the majestic mountains beyond. Derived from the Irish language, "Achadh an Dá Eo," meaning "field of the two yews," Aghadoe is steeped in history, with the ruins of Aghadoe Cathedral dating back to the 7th century. The remnants of the cathedral and the round tower added a cultural layer to the breathtaking scenery.
Our next destination, the Kerry Bog Village, transported us back in time to a 19th-century bog village. Nestled between Killorglin and Glenbeigh, this open-air museum provided a glimpse into the lives of small communities that eked out a living in Ireland's peat bogs during harsh times. Thatched cottages, furnished with authentic antiques, showcased the customs and living conditions of the era, complete with sound effects and figurines. The outdoor displays featured rural farm equipment, and we encountered the renowned Kerry Bog Ponies and Irish Wolfhounds, adding a touch of authenticity to the immersive experience.
Continuing our exploration, we enjoyed the Kells Sheepdogs Show on the Ring of Kerry, skillfully run by a local farmer. Witnessing the agility and intelligence of the sheepdogs highlighted the crucial role of these working animals in rural agriculture.

Valentia Island beckoned with the historical significance of the Telegraph Field, marking the landing site of the first successful transatlantic telegraph cable. The island played a pivotal role in connecting Europe and North America in 1858, reducing communication time from weeks to minutes. The monument on Valentia Island commemorated this groundbreaking achievement, symbolizing a milestone in global telecommunications.
As hunger called, we paused for lunch at Fisherman's Bar and Restaurant in Portmagee, a village on the Iveragh Peninsula along the Ring of Kerry. The picturesque coastal setting, charming fishing harbor, and proximity to the Skellig Islands added charm to our dining experience.
Our journey delved further into history at Derrynane House, the summer residence of Daniel O'Connell, a key figure in Irish political history known as "The Liberator." The mansion, set in Derrynane National Historic Park, showcased O'Connell's contributions to Catholic emancipation and political reform. Exploring the house, gardens, and coastal views offered a glimpse into the life of this influential Irish leader.
The day culminated with a visit to Torc Waterfall, a stunning cascade at the base of Torc Mountain. The 20-meter-high and 110-meter-long waterfall, formed by the Owengarriff River, provided a serene backdrop against the rugged landscape.
After a delightful dinner, our journey through County Kerry concluded with a leisurely stroll through the streets of Killarney. The following day's departure was imminent, but the echoes of County Kerry's beauty and cultural significance lingered, a testament to the richness of our Irish adventure.

Day 09

Killarney to Kinsale

20-Jul-2023

Our journey from Killarney to Kinsale was marked by a day of extensive driving and waiting, with limited opportunities for sightseeing.
One notable stop on our route was the Blarney Woolen Mills, a historic Irish company and a popular tourist destination situated in the village of Blarney, near Cork. Dating back to 1823, the mill complex boasts a rich history in the textile industry. Initially a modest woolen and cotton factory, it eventually grew to become one of Ireland's largest woolen mills. The connection to Blarney Castle adds an enchanting element to the site, as the castle's famous Blarney Stone is believed to bestow the gift of eloquence upon those who kiss it. Despite Fiona's eagerness to follow in her mother's footsteps and kiss the stone, miscommunication from our tour guide and long queues thwarted her plans.
Next on our journey was the renowned Midleton Distillery, known for producing a range of Irish whiskeys, including iconic brands such as Jameson, Redbreast, and Green Spot. However, this particular stopover didn't align with our interests, and we opted to bypass it.
Our day concluded with a visit to Charles Fort, a historical star-shaped fort situated at the southern end of the village of Summer Cove, overlooking Kinsale harbor. Constructed in the late 17th century by architect William Robinson, the fort bears the name of Charles II, the then-king of England, Scotland, and Ireland. Charles Fort played a pivotal role in significant events in Irish history, including the Williamite War and the Irish Civil War. It endured a siege by William of Orange's forces in 1690 and was later burned by anti-Treaty forces in 1922.

Day 10

Kinsale to Kilkenny

21-Jul-2023

Today's first visited town was Cobh, formerly Queenstown, greeted us with its maritime charm on the south coast of County Cork. This port town, steeped in history, was the poignant final port of call for the ill-fated RMS Titanic in 1912, marking a significant chapter in Irish maritime heritage. St. Colman's Cathedral, an awe-inspiring example of French Gothic architecture, stood as a prominent landmark in Cobh. Designed by the renowned architect E.W. Pugin, its pointed arches, ribbed vaults, and flying buttresses reflected a captivating blend of artistry and history. Unfortunately, a wedding ceremony prevented us from exploring the cathedral's interior, leaving us to marvel at its exterior grandeur.
Continuing our journey, we visited Youghal, a town at the mouth of the River Blackwater. Steeped in medieval architecture and rich history, Youghal provided us with an opportunity to visit Moby Dick's Pub, where some of us posed for photos with an artificial wooden leg - an unexpected and amusing encounter.
Wandering through Waterford, a city with a history dating back to the Viking era, we immersed ourselves in its cultural heritage. The King Of Vikings Virtual Reality Experience transported us centuries back in time, providing a fascinating glimpse into the city's historical evolution.
Finally, we reached Kilkenny, renowned for its medieval architecture. Kilkenny Castle, St. Canice's Cathedral, and other historic buildings painted a vivid picture of Ireland's past. The medieval charm of Kilkenny Castle, standing proudly along the River Nore, offered a glimpse into the country's regal history.

Day 11

Kilkenny to Dublin

22-Jul-2023

As our tour of Ireland reached its culmination, there was a subtle sense that our tour organizer had exhausted their repertoire of activities. The fatigue from days of exploration hung in the air, and we were all eager to wrap up this adventure.
Our day began amidst the enchanting surroundings of Kilkenny Castle Park, a testament to centuries of history. The castle, a marvel with its amalgamation of Norman, Gothic, and Renaissance architectural influences, stood proudly, surrounded by meticulously landscaped gardens. The Victorian - era design, complete with ornamental sculptures and water features, transported us to a bygone era. The expansive grounds extended beyond the formal gardens, inviting us to take leisurely strolls through the park, immersing ourselves in the historical ambiance.
Our journey then took an ancient turn as we encountered the Brownshill Portal Tomb Dolmen, a colossal structure dating back to 4000 BCE. This megalithic monument, one of Europe's oldest and largest, showcased the ancient craftsmanship of its builders, leaving us in awe of its sheer magnitude.
Refueling our spirits with a traditional Irish lunch at The Hollywood Inn, a charming pub dating back to 1790, set against the scenic backdrop of the Wicklow Gap, we gathered the energy for our next destination: Glendalough.
Wandering through the glacial valley, Glendalough unfolded its natural beauty and historical treasures before us. The monastic settlement, founded by St. Kevin in the 6th century, spoke volumes about Ireland's early Christian heritage. The well-preserved round tower stood tall, a symbol of protection during Viking raids and a testament to the enduring spirit of the monastic community.
As the day drew to a close, we returned to Dublin, concluding our Irish tour at Draper Rooms.
